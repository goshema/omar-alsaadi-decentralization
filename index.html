The Myth of

as We Know It

A Critical Examination of Bitcoin, Stablecoins, and the Hidden Centralization of Digital Finance
By Omar Alsaadi
Published on May 5, 2025
For those who question the narrative.
Because truth is the foundation of real freedom.

Introduction: The Promise of Freedom

In the aftermath of the 2008 financial crisis, a mysterious figure named Satoshi Nakamoto released a whitepaper titled “Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System.” It promised a radical vision: a decentralized, borderless, censorship-resistant digital currency that would liberate money from banks, governments, and central planners.

For over a decade, this idea has captivated technologists, libertarians, investors, and revolutionaries. Bitcoin was hailed as the first truly sovereign money — not issued by a state, not controlled by a central bank, but secured by math and maintained by a global network of equals.

But what if this vision was never realized? What if, beneath the rhetoric of decentralization, we built a system that is just as centralized as the one it sought to replace — only more opaque, less accountable, and still deeply dependent on the very powers it claims to defy?

This essay argues that decentralization, as currently practiced in cryptocurrency, is largely a myth — one carefully constructed by ideology, obscured by technology, and sustained by the hidden dominance of the United States financial and technological infrastructure.

From Bitcoin’s reliance on NSA cryptography to stablecoins’ entanglement with U.S. Treasury debt, the so-called “decentralized” financial revolution is, in reality, a new chapter in American financial hegemony — digitized, tokenized, and repackaged for the 21st century.

I. The Illusion of Bitcoin: Code, Control, and Concentration

1. Governance by a Handful of Developers

Despite being open-source, Bitcoin Core — the software that defines the protocol — is maintained by a tiny group of core developers. At any given time, fewer than five administrators have the power to merge code changes, approve updates, and shape the future of the network.

While no one can force nodes to upgrade, in practice, the community follows the lead of this small, unelected group — many of whom are funded by private corporations like Block (formerly Square) or academic institutions tied to U.S. interests. This is not decentralized governance. It is technocratic oligarchy — a quiet concentration of power masked by open-source idealism.

2. Mining: The Myth of Distributed Security

Bitcoin’s proof-of-work system is supposed to be secure because mining is distributed. In reality, six mining pools — based in the U.S., China, and Europe — have controlled over 95% of the hash power for years. This means that a handful of entities could, in theory, collude to reverse transactions or censor blocks.

Moreover, mining is dominated by ASIC chips produced by a few companies (like Bitmain), and electricity access is concentrated in regions with cheap power — often under state influence. The idea that “anyone can mine” is a fantasy. The reality is industrial-scale mining controlled by a few players.

3. Ownership: Digital Feudalism

On-chain data consistently shows that the top 1.86% of Bitcoin addresses hold over 90% of all BTC. This level of wealth concentration exceeds even the most unequal traditional economies.

Entities like MicroStrategy, Tesla, and anonymous early miners control vast reserves. When these whales move, the market trembles. This is not financial democracy — it is digital feudalism, where a small elite holds disproportionate power over supply, price, and narrative.

4. Dependence on U.S. Infrastructure

Bitcoin runs on:

  • SHA-256, a cryptographic hash function developed by the U.S. National Security Agency (NSA).
  • The global internet, largely routed through U.S.-controlled infrastructure.
  • U.S. dollar pricing, with nearly all trading pairs denominated in USD.
  • Monetary policy set by the Federal Reserve, which flooded the world with cheap money — inflating Bitcoin’s price.

Bitcoin may oppose central banks in theory, but in practice, it depends on the Fed’s liquidity and the U.S. tech stack to function. Remove these, and the system collapses.

II. Stablecoins: The Privatization of the Dollar Empire

If Bitcoin is the myth of decentralized money, stablecoins are the myth of decentralized finance.

Stablecoins like USDT (Tether) and USDC (Circle) claim to offer “digital dollars” — fast, global, and open. But they are, in fact, a privatized extension of the U.S. financial system — one that deepens, rather than disrupts, dollar dominance.

1. Backed by U.S. Treasuries — Not Independence

Most dollar-pegged stablecoins are now primarily backed by U.S. Treasury bonds, not cash. This means:

  • Every new stablecoin issued increases demand for U.S. government debt.
  • The U.S. Treasury benefits from private-sector financing of its deficit.
  • The system creates a feedback loop: more stablecoins → more Treasury demand → lower borrowing costs → more deficit spending.

This is not de-dollarization. It is dollarization via crypto — what some economists call the “Stablecoin Standard.”

2. Controlled by Permission

Stablecoin issuers are regulated U.S. companies. They:

  • Require KYC/AML compliance.
  • Can freeze or blacklist wallets.
  • Operate under Treasury Department oversight.

In 2021, the U.S. Treasury proposed rules requiring stablecoin transactions over $10 to be reported — a move toward full surveillance. There is no financial privacy, and no immunity from state control.

When the SEC sued Binance, it froze $24 million in USDC — proving that stablecoins are not beyond government reach.

3. A Fragile House of Cards

Stablecoins promise $1 = $1. But this peg depends on:

  • Confidence in U.S. Treasuries.
  • Stability of the banking system (e.g., USDC lost its peg when Silicon Valley Bank collapsed).
  • Continuous demand for dollar assets.

If the U.S. debt crisis worsens — say, due to a $4 trillion “Big Beautiful Bill” under a future Trump administration — the foundation cracks. Stablecoins don’t solve the problem. They amplify it.

III. The U.S. Strategy: Tokenizing the Empire

The U.S. government did not fight crypto — it co-opted it.

Rather than banning stablecoins, Washington is encouraging their growth — because they:

  • Extend dollar dominance into the digital economy.
  • Finance U.S. debt through private-sector demand.
  • Bypass rivals like China’s digital yuan (e-CNY), which offers an alternative but comes with state surveillance.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent was explicit:

“A thriving stablecoin ecosystem will drive demand from the private sector for US Treasuries, which back stablecoins.”
This is not a bug. It is a feature of U.S. financial strategy.

While countries like China, Russia, and members of BRICS seek to de-dollarize, the U.S. is tokenizing the dollar — embedding it into blockchain, DeFi, and global crypto trade. The result? The dollar remains the default, even in systems designed to escape it.

IV. The Digital Yuan: A Mirror, Not a Solution

China’s digital yuan (e-CNY) is often presented as the anti-Bitcoin — a state-controlled CBDC. But it is also a warning.

e-CNY offers:

  • Fast, cheap transactions.
  • No reliance on SWIFT.
  • Full government control — including the ability to freeze accounts, expire money, and track every transaction.

Many nations hesitate to adopt it — not because it’s inefficient, but because it trades U.S. financial dominance for Chinese surveillance finance.

Yet, ironically, U.S. stablecoins are not truly free either. They are censorable, traceable, and permissioned — just under a different model.

The choice is not between freedom and control, but between two forms of centralized power:

  • American-style: private companies, light regulation, but ultimate state oversight.
  • Chinese-style: direct state control, no pretense of decentralization.

Neither offers true financial sovereignty.

V. Toward a Real Decentralization?

So what would true decentralization look like?

It would require:

  • Truly open governance — not 5 devs, but decentralized decision-making via transparent, on-chain voting.
  • Distributed mining and validation — not pools, but millions of small participants.
  • Wealth dispersion — mechanisms to prevent hoarding and oligarchy.
  • Non-sovereign backing — stablecoins tied to commodity baskets, not U.S. debt.
  • Independent infrastructure — open-source cryptography not tied to state agencies, mesh networks, local energy.

Projects like Monero, Zcash, Decentralized Identity, and peer-to-peer exchanges point the way — but they remain marginal.

Until then, decentralization remains a marketing slogan, not a reality.

Conclusion: The Myth and the Mission

The myth of decentralization is not harmless. It is dangerous — because it distracts us from the real work of building truly open, fair, and resilient systems.

Bitcoin and stablecoins did not break the system. They rebuilt it in digital form — with the same power structures, just less transparency.

The U.S. dollar is not dying. It is evolving — from paper to digital, from banks to blockchains, from Treasuries to stablecoins.

And the greatest irony? The revolution was monetized — and the empire issued the receipt.

But the critique you’ve raised — about control, concentration, and dependency — is the first step toward something real.

Because only when we admit the myth can we begin to build the truth.

And perhaps, one day, we’ll create a financial system that is not just called decentralized — but actually is.

Until then, we must keep asking: Who really controls the keys? Who holds the power? And who benefits from the story we’re being told?

The answers may not be on the blockchain. But they are the only ones that matter.
